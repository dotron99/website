Preserving Heritage in Pixels: Reconstructing the A Famosa Fortress

Dr. Mohamad Izani·20 June 2026·7 min read

My doctoral work asked a deceptively simple question: how do you rebuild something that no longer exists? The A Famosa Fortress in Melaka, Malaysia, survives today as a single gate. Everything else must be inferred. That gap between what remains and what once stood is where digital heritage lives.

It is tempting to think of a 3D reconstruction as an act of modelling — a matter of skill in the software. In truth, the modelling is the last and easiest step. The hard work is evidence: gathering it, weighing it, and being honest about how much of the final image is fact and how much is careful conjecture.

Starting from fragments

For A Famosa, the sources were scattered and inconsistent — colonial-era descriptions, old maps, sketches drawn by visitors with varying skill and agendas, and the physical remains of the surviving gate. No two accounts agreed on scale. Some were written by people who never saw the fortress. Reconciling them is less like copying a blueprint and more like assembling a case from partial testimony.

We used geometric analysis to test which layouts were even possible given the surviving evidence, comparing textual and visual data rather than trusting any single source. The goal was not the most beautiful reconstruction, but the most defensible one — a model a historian could interrogate, not just admire.

A digital reconstruction is an argument, not a photograph. Its value lies in showing its reasoning, not hiding it.

The ethics of certainty

This raises a question that every heritage project must answer: how do you represent uncertainty? A photorealistic render is persuasive precisely because it looks complete. But when half the structure is inferred, a seamless image can quietly mislead. Over the years I have come to believe that heritage visualisation carries a duty to distinguish the known from the reconstructed — through documentation, through visual cues, or simply through honest accompanying text.

This becomes even more pressing with sacred or culturally sensitive sites. When my collaborators and I reconstructed mosques in virtual reality, fidelity was not only a technical target but a matter of respect. Getting a proportion wrong is not a rendering error; it is a failure of care toward the people for whom the place holds meaning.

Why rebuild at all?

People sometimes ask whether digital reconstruction is worth the effort when the original is gone. I think it is, for three reasons. It preserves knowledge that would otherwise decay with the last physical fragment. It makes heritage accessible — a student in another country can walk through a fortress that no longer stands. And it invites new questions, because building a model forces you to confront every assumption you would otherwise leave comfortably vague.

From A Famosa we went on to augmented-reality applications that let visitors stand on the original site and see the fortress rise around them, and to mobile game-based learning that turned the history into something young people could explore rather than memorise. The reconstruction was never the endpoint. It was the foundation for everything else.

Where generative AI fits

Today, generative AI is changing the texture of this work. It can propose reconstructions of missing detail, generate plausible period materials, and accelerate the tedious middle of the pipeline. This is powerful and genuinely useful. It is also exactly where the discipline of evidence matters most. An AI-generated wall that looks right is not the same as a wall the sources can support. Used well, these tools speed up heritage work; used carelessly, they manufacture a convincing past that never existed.

That is the balance I keep returning to. The pixels are easy. The responsibility of deciding what they claim is the real craft.
