From 2D to 3D: Building an AI-Augmented Animation Pipeline

Dr. Mohamad Izani·28 May 2026·6 min read

Generative AI has arrived in the animation studio, and the loudest voices tend toward two extremes: it will replace animators, or it is a toy that produces uncanny mush. Having spent the last two years building and testing AI-augmented pipelines, I find both claims miss the point. The interesting question is not whether AI can animate. It is which parts of the workflow it should touch.

High-fidelity animation is not a single task. It is a chain of them — concept, modelling, rigging, texturing, motion, lighting, compositing. Generative tools do not perform equally well across that chain, and treating them as a single magic button is the fastest way to be disappointed.

Where AI genuinely helps

In our work reimagining 2D assets as 3D, the clearest wins came in the early and middle stages. AI accelerates ideation, expands a single concept into dozens of variations in minutes, and handles the laborious in-between work that used to consume hours. For studios without large teams — and for students learning the craft — this lowers the barrier to producing something ambitious.

Texture and material generation is another strong fit. So is rapid previsualisation: getting a rough version of a shot in front of a director early, when changing direction is still cheap. These are places where speed matters more than final polish, and where a good-enough result unblocks the next decision.

AI is best at the drafts and the drudgery. It is weakest exactly where animation earns its emotional power — timing, intention and restraint.

Where the artist still leads

The principles that make animation move an audience — anticipation, timing, weight, the deliberate exaggeration that reads as truth — are still authored by people. When we evaluated AI-generated animation against these classical principles, the pattern was consistent. The tools produce motion that is plausible frame to frame but often hollow across a sequence. It moves without meaning to.

This is not a flaw to be patched in the next release. It reflects what the models optimise for. They predict what is likely, and great animation is frequently built on the unlikely, precise choice — the held pause, the overshoot, the beat of stillness before an action. An artist makes those choices on purpose. A model averages them away.

Designing the pipeline around that truth

So the pipeline we advocate is not "AI does the animation." It is closer to a relay. AI carries the work through the stages where volume and speed dominate — concept exploration, asset generation, rough motion, cleanup. The artist takes the baton at every stage where judgement and intention decide quality, and above all owns the final performance. The tool amplifies the animator; it does not replace them.

Getting this handoff right requires measurement, not vibes. We built assessment models to score AI-generated output on concrete dimensions rather than gut feeling, so a team can decide objectively where the machine's work is ready to keep and where it needs a human pass. Without that, studios either over-trust the output or reject it wholesale, and both are expensive mistakes.

What this means for training the next generation

For the students I teach, the lesson is reassuring rather than threatening. The fundamentals matter more than ever. A student who understands the principles of motion can direct these tools; one who does not will be directed by them, and it will show. The pipeline of the near future belongs to artists who can wield AI as a powerful, imperfect collaborator — knowing precisely when to let it run, and when to take the pencil back.

That, to me, is the honest promise of AI in animation. Not the end of the animator, but the amplification of the ones who understand their craft.
