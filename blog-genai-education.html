Teaching in the Age of Generative AI: Lessons from Training 5,000 Professionals

Dr. Mohamad Izani·15 July 2026·6 min read

Over the past three years, through AI4LIFE, I have run more than fifty training sessions and worked with over five thousand educators, executives and creative professionals. The single clearest lesson is this: the barrier to adopting generative AI is rarely the technology. It is confidence, context and support.

When I began delivering these workshops, I expected the hardest part to be the tools themselves — the prompts, the settings, the jargon. In practice, most people grasp the mechanics within an hour. What separates those who go on to use AI daily from those who quietly abandon it is something less technical: whether they can see the tool working on their problem, in their language, in front of them.

Educators are not one audience

In a recent study I led on global educator typologies for ChatGPT adoption, one finding stood out. Educators do not divide neatly into "adopters" and "resisters." They cluster into distinct groups, each with different needs — the curious experimenter who lacks time, the cautious sceptic who needs evidence, the enthusiast who needs guardrails, the overwhelmed newcomer who needs a starting point. Treating them as a single audience is why so much AI training fails to land.

Once you recognise these groups, the design of a session changes. The sceptic needs data and a worked example from a trusted peer. The newcomer needs a small, safe first win. The enthusiast needs to understand ethics and limits before they over-rely on the output. The same forty-minute demonstration cannot serve all of them equally, and pretending otherwise wastes everyone's time.

The support gap

The most consistent request I hear is not for more features. It is for someone to help translate the general capability of a tool into the specific shape of a job. A lecturer does not want to know that a model "can generate text." They want to see it draft a rubric aligned to their learning outcomes, in their discipline, and then be shown how to check it critically. The gap between capability and application is where adoption stalls, and it is exactly where a good trainer earns their place.

Capability is abundant and free. The scarce resource is context — knowing which task, in which workflow, is worth handing to a machine.

Teach judgement, not just prompts

Early on, I taught prompting as a set of tricks. I have since shifted the emphasis. Prompts change with every model release; judgement does not. The durable skills are knowing when the output is good enough, when it is subtly wrong, and when the task should never have been delegated in the first place. In creative and academic work especially, the ability to critique AI output is more valuable than the ability to produce it.

This is why every session I run now ends with hands-on labs on participants' real material, not toy examples. People remember the moment the tool solved something they actually cared about. That memory, more than any slide, is what brings them back the next week.

What I would tell any institution

If you are leading AI adoption in a university or company, resist the urge to start with a policy document or a platform licence. Start with people. Find the groups, meet each where they are, give them a small real win, and build the support structure that turns a one-off workshop into a habit. The organisations I have seen lift AI adoption meaningfully — by our measures, around forty per cent — did not have better tools than everyone else. They had better follow-through.

Generative AI will keep changing. The teaching principles will not. Meet people where they are, show the tool on their problem, and teach them to judge what comes back. Everything else is detail.
